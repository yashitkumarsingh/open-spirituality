# Source Policy

The **Open Spirituality** project holds a non-negotiable principle: **“Open scholarship, not copied spirituality.”** This document sets forth our legal, ethical, and scholarly guidelines for handling source materials, copyright, and lineage traditions.

---

## 1. Respect for Intellectual Property & Copyrights

We do **not** copy modern translations, commentaries, or course material into this repository unless explicit written permission exists from the author or copyright holder. 
Many standard works on Kashmir Shaivism and the Vijñāna Bhairava Tantra are protected by active copyrights:
- **Christopher Wallis (Hareesh)**: Translations and concordances are copyrighted and may not be reproduced or distributed without permission.
- **Lakshmanjoo Academy**: Audio recordings, transcriptions, and books containing Swami Lakshmanjoo’s translations and commentary are copyrighted.
- **Motilal Banarsidass Editions**: Works by Jaideva Singh, Bettina Bäumer, Lilian Silburn, and others are commercially published and copyrighted.

### Our Policy:
1. **No direct copying**: We do not include blocks of copyrighted text.
2. **References, not reproductions**: We compare translations, interpretations, and notes using standard citation methods, referencing our bibliography.
3. **Fair Use for Comparative Quotes**: If a short quote (a few words or a single sentence) is necessary to highlight a specific scholarly dispute or linguistic interpretation, it must be explicitly cited with the scholar's name and publication year, strictly conforming to the "Fair Use" doctrine for educational/research purposes.

---

## 2. Sanskrit Base Text Policy

Our primary base text is:
- **Kashmir Series of Texts and Studies (KSTS), No. IX (1918)**: Edited by Mukund Rām Shāstrī, featuring the *Kaumadī* commentary by Ānanda Bhaṭṭa.

This edition is in the public domain globally. We also consult:
- **Muktabodha Digital Library**: A crucial repository of Śaiva, Śākta, and Tantric texts. We link to Muktabodha's catalog and cite their transcriptions where relevant. Note that while Muktabodha makes texts "freely available" for research, we respect their terms of use and do not bulk-copy their proprietary digital databases.

---

## 3. Original Open Translations

To provide a fully open resource, we generate our own **Open Translations** for this repository.
- **Methodology**: Our translation is built by analyzing the Sanskrit grammar word-by-word, consulting dictionaries (Apte, Monier-Williams), and checking traditional commentaries (like the *Kaumadī*).
- **Goal**: Our translation aims to be grammatically accurate, philosophically clear, and modern, without copying any copyrighted translations.
- **License**: All original translations, grammatical tables, and practice notes are released under the [Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International (CC BY-SA 4.0)](../../LICENSE.md) license.

---

## 4. Distinction Between Traditional Lineages and Modern Innovations

To maintain academic rigor and practitioner utility, each verse/sutra file divides commentary into clear, separate sections:
- **Literal Reading**: A plain, direct translation of what the Sanskrit grammar states, stripped of theological elaboration.
- **Philosophical Meaning**: The traditional theological context within the Trika / Kashmir Shaivism system, referencing primary authors like Abhinavagupta, Kṣemarāja, and Ānanda Bhaṭṭa.
- **Modern Contemplative/Practice Instruction**: How the verse is used as a practice in modern settings, written in clean, non-dogmatic language.
- **Practice Cautions**: A section dedicated to physical and psychological safety, avoiding medical claims or forced somatic strain.
