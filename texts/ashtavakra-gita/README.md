## Context & Overview

The *Aṣṭāvakra Gītā* (c. 500 BCE - 800 CE) is a classical scripture of **Advaita Vedanta** (nondualism). Framed as a dialogue between the sage Aṣṭāvakra and the king Janaka of Mithila, it represents one of the most direct, uncompromising expositions of pure nondual reality (*ajātavāda* and *dṛṣṭi-sṛṣṭi-vāda*). 

Unlike the Bhagavad Gita, which incorporates karma, bhakti, and progressive paths of yoga, the Ashtavakra Gita rejects all paths, practices, rituals, and divisions of time and space, declaring the Self (*Ātman*) to be already eternally free, pure consciousness, and the witness (*sākṣī*) of all manifestation.

The text opens with King Janaka asking three core questions:
1. How is saving knowledge (*jñāna*) attained?
2. How is liberation (*mukti*) realized?
3. How is dispassion (*vairāgya*) acquired?

Sage Aṣṭāvakra's immediate reply forms the rest of the text, leading Janaka to immediate, direct self-recognition.

## Important Disclaimers

> [!WARNING]
> **Spiritual and Psychological Cautions**:
> The Ashtavakra Gita utilizes an uncompromising, absolute (*paramārthika*) perspective. It declares that there is no bondage, no liberation, no practice, and no individual doer. Practicing this text as a conceptual dogma without proper maturity can lead to intellectual self-deception, spiritual bypassing, or dry intellectualism (pseudo-liberation). Seekers are advised to approach these contemplations as experiential pointers rather than theoretical excuses to ignore moral, somatic, or psychological integration.
